Randomized Controlled Trial of Yogic Meditation Techniques for Patients With Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

By David S. Shannahoff-Khalsa, Leslie E. Ray, MS, MFCC, Saul Levine, MD, Christopher C. Gallen, MD, PhD, Barry J. Schwartz, PhD, and John J. Sidorowich, PhD

CNS Spectrums - Volume 4, Number 12 - December 1999

Abstract Overview

This study aimed to compare the efficacy of two meditation protocols for treating patients with Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD). Patients were randomized into two groups: one employing a Kundalini Yoga (KY) meditation protocol, and the other using a Relaxation Response plus Mindfulness Meditation (RR+MM) technique. Over 3 months, the KY group showed significantly greater improvements on several OCD and mood scales. After this initial phase, both groups utilized the KY techniques for an additional year. The final merged group demonstrated substantial and statistically significant improvements across all measured psychological scales at 15 months, suggesting that Kundalini Yoga techniques are effective in the treatment of OCD.

Study Design & Participant Flow

The trial recruited patients diagnosed with OCD according to DSM-III-R criteria. Initially, 50 adults and 2 adolescents were screened. Of these, 22 adults and 1 adolescent fulfilled initial criteria and were allocated to treatment after pretreatment assessment. Ultimately, 21 adults and 1 adolescent girl (Patient 11, aged 16) actually participated and entered randomization.

Participants were then randomized and matched for sex, age, and medication status into two groups for Phase 1:

Interventions (Phase 1 - First 3 Months):

  • Group 1 (Kundalini Yoga - KY): Initially 10 adults and 1 adolescent (Patient 11). They practiced a specific Kundalini Yoga meditation protocol.
  • Group 2 (Relaxation Response + Mindfulness Meditation - RR+MM): Initially 11 adults. They practiced the Relaxation Response technique combined with Mindfulness Meditation.

Assessment Measures:

Patients were assessed at baseline and at 3-month intervals using standardized psychological scales, including:

  • Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS) - Primary outcome measure
  • Symptoms Checklist-90-Revised Obsessive Compulsive (SCL-90-R OC)
  • Global Severity Index (SCL-90-R GSI)
  • Profile of Moods Scale (POMS)
  • Perceived Stress Scale (PSS)
  • Purpose in Life (PIL) Test

The initial trial phase was 3 months. If one protocol proved superior, the groups would merge, and all participants would use the more effective technique for an extended period (up to 15 months total).

The Kundalini Yoga (KY) Protocol for OCD

The Kundalini Yoga protocol used in this study was a comprehensive set of techniques, taught by an expert with extensive experience. The full protocol, requiring approximately 1 hour to complete daily, included eight primary techniques and three non-mandatory ones. A key component, purported to be specific for treating OCD, involved a particular breathing exercise (pranayama):

  • OCD-Specific Breathing Technique:
    • The right nostril is blocked (using a thumb tip or a secure plug).
    • Slow, deep inspiration is performed through the left nostril.
    • This is followed by breath retention.
    • Then, a slow, complete expiration is done through the left nostril.
    • Finally, a long period of holding the breath out (suspension after exhalation) is maintained.
    • This entire pattern (inhalation, retention, exhalation, suspension) is continued for a maximum of 31 minutes.
    • Patients were instructed to gradually work towards making each of the four phases of the breath cycle last for 15 seconds, resulting in a complete breath cycle of 1 minute.

The paper notes that this left-nostril breathing technique is believed by yogis to selectively stimulate the right hemisphere of the brain, which has been identified in other research as potentially having abnormalities in individuals with OCD. Other techniques in the protocol were also claimed to be useful for treating anxiety, anger, and fear.

Phase 1: Initial 3-Month Comparison & Attrition

During the first 3-month period, each group experienced attrition. One adult woman in Group 1 (KY) was eliminated due to increasing her benzodiazepine dosage. Additionally, 3 adults dropped out of Group 1 (KY), and the adolescent girl (Patient 11) also withdrew from this group. Group 2 (RR+MM) also had 3 adult dropouts.

This left 7 adults completing the 3-month therapy in Group 1 (KY) and 7 adults completing in Group 2 (RR+MM). The mean baseline Y-BOCS scores for these completers were not significantly different between groups (KY: 28.7; RR+MM: 26.1).

After the first 3 months of therapy, the Kundalini Yoga (KY) group demonstrated significantly greater improvements compared to the Relaxation Response + Mindfulness Meditation (RR+MM) group on the primary outcome measure, the Y-BOCS, as well as other scales.

Y-BOCS Percentage Improvement at 3 Months (Completers, N=7 per group)

The KY group (N=7) showed a 45.8% improvement (mean Y-BOCS change from 28.7 to 15.6; P<.001), while the RR+MM group (N=7) showed a 12.6% improvement (mean Y-BOCS change from 26.1 to 22.8; P=.17, not statistically significant). Lower scores on Y-BOCS indicate improvement.

Within-group analysis for these completers showed that Group 1 (KY) significantly improved on all six assessment scales (Y-BOCS, SCL-90-R OC, SCL-90-R GSI, POMS, PSS, PIL; P < .01 to P < .001), whereas Group 2 (RR+MM) showed no significant improvements on any scale during this initial 3-month period.

Phase 2: Long-Term Effects of Kundalini Yoga (Merged Group) & Attrition

Based on the superior results of the KY protocol in Phase 1, the groups were merged. The 7 completers from Group 2 (RR+MM) switched to the KY protocol. One adult woman from Group 1 who had previously withdrawn (Patient 8) chose to re-enter. Additionally, three new candidates (2 women, 1 adolescent boy with Tourette Syndrome) who couldn't participate earlier joined, starting the KY protocol. Thus, 18 patients started Phase 2 using the KY protocol.

During Phase 2 (months 3 to 15), there was further attrition. Three patients (Patients 1, 10, and 17) withdrew after several weeks into Phase 2. The paper presents long-term data for participants who completed tests for at least two 3-month intervals. Ultimately, 11 patients completed the full 15 months of the study.

Overall Improvement at 15 Months (Completers, N=11)

Comparison with Other OCD Treatments

The paper discusses the efficacy of the Kundalini Yoga (KY) protocol in the context of other established treatments for OCD, primarily pharmacotherapy (medications like Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors - SRIs) and Behavior Therapy (BT), which often includes Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP).

Pharmacotherapy (SRIs)

The authors compare the Y-BOCS improvements seen with KY to those reported in studies of SRIs:

  • A multicenter fluvoxamine study showed a 20.7% Y-BOCS improvement for the active drug (versus 7% for placebo).
  • A review of four multicenter trials for clomipramine, fluoxetine, fluvoxamine, and sertraline found Y-BOCS improvements ranging from 23% to 44% for the best dose comparisons.
  • Placebo effects in SRI trials are typically in the range of 5% to 10% improvement on the Y-BOCS.

In contrast, the KY protocol in this study demonstrated:

  • 45.8% Y-BOCS improvement at 3 months.
  • 53% Y-BOCS improvement at 15 months for the completers.

The Relaxation Response + Mindfulness (RR+MM) control group in this study showed a 12.6% improvement at 3 months, which was not statistically significant and was considered by the authors to be potentially a placebo-level effect. The KY group's improvement significantly surpassed this.

Behavior Therapy (BT / ERP)

The paper references studies on BT/ERP, noting:

  • BT/ERP can lead to 60% to 90% of patients being rated as "much improved."
  • Approximately 75% of OCD patients are reported to "get control of their symptoms" with BT, with about 83% able to complete the therapy.
  • However, dropout rates can be significant (e.g., one-quarter refusing or dropping out early), and a portion of those who complete BT may not improve (around 25%) or may require "booster" sessions (around 30% of improved patients).
  • The authors also point out that many historical BT studies focused on cleaning and checking rituals (often easier to treat with BT) and did not always use the Y-BOCS. Furthermore, the acute phase of ERP can increase fear and anxiety, affecting compliance.

While a direct statistical comparison of Y-BOCS percentage improvement between this KY study and historical BT/ERP averages is not explicitly drawn in the same way as with SRIs, the KY protocol's 53% improvement at 15 months with good retention and reduced medication is presented as a very positive outcome. The group therapy format of KY also offers cost and therapist time advantages.

Kundalini Yoga vs. Placebo/Attention

The fact that the KY group showed significant improvements while the RR+MM control group (also a meditation-based intervention involving attention and group meetings) did not, strongly suggests that the benefits of KY were due to therapy-specific factors rather than just placebo effects or the general effects of attention and group support.

Medication Reduction

An important finding was the impact on medication usage. Of the 12 patients who entered the protocol while taking medication for OCD, 6 completed the full 15-month study. Among these 6 completers:

  • Three patients (50%) became free of medication for a minimum of 3 months prior to the study's end.
  • The other three patients in this subgroup were able to reduce their medication dosage.

Key Conclusion

The study robustly demonstrates that the specific Kundalini Yoga meditation techniques employed are effective in the treatment of Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder. The improvements were not only statistically significant but also clinically meaningful, sustained over a long period, and superior to a credible control meditation protocol (Relaxation Response + Mindfulness). The authors suggest that the efficacy may be due to therapy-specific factors within the KY protocol, potentially related to its effects on brain hemisphere activity, rather than a placebo effect or general attention.
